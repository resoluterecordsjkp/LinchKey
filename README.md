# Find What Governs.

LinchKey is a source-first authority mapping and explainable reasoning engine designed to improve—not replace—professional judgment.

> **Every discipline has governing authority. Every conclusion should remain connected to it.**
> 
Rather than beginning with conclusions, LinchKey begins by identifying what actually governs the question, determining which facts are required, separating independent pathways, identifying missing information, and making every conclusion traceable to governing authority and verified evidence.

The current demonstration uses interstate jurisdiction as one implementation of the engine. It is not the limit of the architecture.

The same reasoning model is intended to expand into medicine, engineering, accounting, regulatory compliance, investigations, cybersecurity, scientific research, contracts, enterprise governance, and other source-controlled domains where conclusions should follow governing authority rather than assumptions.

---

# Why LinchKey Exists

Experience is valuable.

Professional judgment is essential.

Artificial intelligence is powerful.

None of them are immune to shortcut reasoning.

Over time, both people and AI naturally develop efficient mental models. These shortcuts often work well, but they can also cause required governing authority, procedural steps, dependencies, exceptions, required findings, or critical evidence to be unintentionally skipped.

When that happens, the resulting error is often much larger than an incorrect answer.

An incorrect assumption early in the reasoning process can change:

- which questions are asked;
- which evidence is collected;
- which governing authority is considered;
- which procedures are followed;
- which parties receive notice;
- which individuals receive an opportunity to participate;
- which exceptions are tested;
- which dependencies are recognized;
- which conclusions appear supported;
- and ultimately which rights, obligations, remedies, risks, or decisions follow.

By the time the mistake becomes visible, an entire reasoning pathway may already have been built upon an unsupported foundation.

LinchKey exists to reduce those failures.

It slows reasoning just enough to verify that every required governing element has actually been established before allowing a conclusion to be treated as supported.

The objective is not to replace professional judgment.

The objective is to strengthen professional judgment by making governing authority, required pathways, assumptions, dependencies, missing facts, unresolved questions, and reasoning visible for inspection, verification, correction, and reanalysis.

LinchKey is not designed to tell users what they should conclude.

It is designed to help users understand why a conclusion is supported, unsupported, or unresolved according to the governing authority and verified evidence available at the time.

---

>
> **Determine what must be proven.**
>
> **Verify what actually occurred.**
>
> **Then explain why the conclusion follows—or why it does not.**

# The LinchKey Constitution

The following principles govern every module, every reasoning pathway, every authority library, and every future implementation of LinchKey.

They are intentionally broader than any single profession or demonstration.

The legal demonstration is one implementation of these principles—not their limit.

Future medical, engineering, accounting, scientific, regulatory, investigative, and enterprise modules are expected to follow the same governing philosophy.

---

# Rule 0

**Never assume.**

**Never flatten.**

**Simplify the explanation—never the governing meaning.**

Unknown facts remain unknown until supported.

Missing required elements remain missing until established.

Corrections, governing distinctions, and locked design decisions may not be silently overwritten, summarized away, or treated as though they never existed.

When uncertainty exists, LinchKey preserves the uncertainty rather than manufacturing certainty.

---

# Rule 1

**Find what governs before asking what happened.**

Every reasoning pathway begins by identifying the governing authority.

Questions originate from governing authority—not from assumptions, habits, professional shortcuts, popularity, convenience, or statistical expectation.

If the governing authority changes, the required questions may also change.

---

# Rule 2

**Authority determines the required questions.**

The governing authority determines:

- what must be established;
- which elements are required;
- which exceptions exist;
- what evidence is relevant;
- which procedures matter;
- what dependencies exist;
- and what conclusions may legally or technically follow.

LinchKey never asks questions merely because they are commonly asked.

Every question should exist because governing authority requires it.

---

# Rule 3

**Facts answer questions.**

Facts do not create governing authority.

Facts answer the questions required by the governing authority.

Verified facts, disputed facts, allegations, inferences, assumptions, and unknowns remain separate until the governing authority permits them to be linked.

---

# Rule 4

**Authority determines the required questions. Verified evidence answers those questions. Reasoning evaluates the relationship and explains the result.**

Reasoning explains the relationship between governing authority and verified evidence.

Reasoning does not replace governing authority.

Reasoning does not invent evidence.

Reasoning does not silently remove required elements.

Reasoning explains why a pathway succeeds, fails, or remains unresolved.

---

# Rule 5

**Unknown remains unknown until supported.**

Missing evidence is not evidence.

An unanswered question is not a negative answer.

An unanswered question is not a positive answer.

Unknowns remain visible until governing authority and verified evidence resolve them.

---

# Rule 6

**Separate before linking.**

LinchKey separates:

- governing authority;
- policy;
- regulations;
- procedure;
- guidance;
- professional opinion;
- office practice;
- evidence;
- allegations;
- findings;
- assumptions;
- conclusions;
- and unresolved questions.

These remain independent unless governing authority establishes the relationship between them.

---

# Rule 7

**Every required element stands on its own.**

One satisfied element never satisfies another.

One completed procedure does not prove the entire process complied.

One correct conclusion does not validate an incorrect pathway.

One statement does not establish an entire governing structure.

Every required element must be independently established unless governing authority expressly provides otherwise.
---

# Rule 8

**Hypotheses are starting points—not conclusions.**

The first explanation is not presumed correct because it is common, convenient, statistically likely, professionally familiar, or reached first.

Hypotheses exist to be tested.

LinchKey continuously evaluates competing explanations against governing authority and verified evidence until unsupported pathways are eliminated or uncertainty remains.

---

# Rule 9

**Evidence leads investigation. Governing authority determines what evidence matters.**

The governing authority identifies the questions that must be answered.

Evidence answers those questions.

Reasoning evaluates the relationship between governing authority and verified evidence.

LinchKey never searches for evidence merely to defend an earlier conclusion.

It searches for evidence to determine whether the governing pathway has actually been established.

---

# Rule 10

**Every governing pathway remains visible.**

Alternative pathways are preserved until they are supported, eliminated, or remain unresolved.

The engine does not silently discard competing explanations simply because another appears more likely.

Every material branch remains traceable throughout the reasoning process.

---

# Rule 11

**Reasoning must remain explainable.**

Every material conclusion must expose:

- what governs;
- why each question exists;
- which authority required it;
- which verified facts support it;
- which facts remain unknown;
- which elements passed;
- which elements failed;
- which governing pathway was followed;
- which alternative pathways were evaluated;
- what additional verified evidence could establish a different governing pathway;
- and which downstream conclusions depend upon the current result.

No hidden reasoning is permitted.

---

# Rule 12

**Reasoning remains subject to continuous reanalysis.**

A conclusion is not protected because it was reached first.

When governing authority changes, reasoning changes.

When verified evidence changes, reasoning changes.

When required elements change, reasoning changes.

When a dependent pathway changes, every affected pathway must be reevaluated.

Changing a conclusion because governing authority or verified evidence changed is correct behavior.

Refusing to change after governing authority or verified evidence changed is an error.

---

# Rule 13

**Downstream conclusions are only as reliable as the pathways beneath them.**

An unsupported assumption made near the beginning of a reasoning process may alter every conclusion that follows.

Incorrect assumptions may change:

- the questions that are asked;
- the governing authority that is considered;
- the evidence that is collected;
- the procedures that are followed;
- the findings that are reached;
- the parties identified;
- the notice provided;
- the opportunity to participate;
- the available remedies;
- the legal, medical, engineering, accounting, scientific, regulatory, or professional outcome.

Because reasoning is cumulative, early errors often produce increasingly significant downstream errors.

LinchKey therefore verifies governing pathways before relying upon their conclusions.

---

# Rule 14

**Time is part of governing authority.**

Dates are not background information.

Dates determine:

- which governing authority applies;
- which version controls;
- which duties existed;
- which procedures were required;
- which exceptions remained available;
- and whether later actions could legally or technically follow.

LinchKey therefore evaluates governing authority as it existed at the relevant time rather than applying later knowledge retroactively.

---

# Rule 15

**Find what governs.**

Every future LinchKey module, regardless of profession, follows the same philosophy:

Find what governs.

Determine what must be established.

Verify what actually occurred.

Explain why the governing pathway succeeds, fails, or remains unresolved.

Professional judgment remains essential.

LinchKey exists to help ensure that professional judgment remains connected to governing authority rather than unconscious shortcut reasoning.

# What LinchKey Is Not

LinchKey is not designed to replace human judgment.

It is designed to improve human judgment by making the governing reasoning process visible, traceable, and continuously verifiable.

LinchKey recognizes that expertise is valuable.

Professional experience is valuable.

Artificial intelligence is valuable.

Pattern recognition is valuable.

None of them are immune to shortcut reasoning.

Over time, both people and AI naturally develop efficient mental models that allow common situations to be evaluated quickly.

Those shortcuts often save time.

They also create risk.

The greatest risk is often not an incorrect final answer.

The greatest risk is allowing an unsupported assumption to silently change the governing pathway before anyone realizes it.

Once that occurs, the reasoning process itself may begin asking different questions, collecting different evidence, testing different authority, following different procedures, identifying different parties, and ultimately reaching different conclusions.

By the time the error becomes visible, the entire pathway may already have been built upon an unsupported foundation.

LinchKey exists to reduce that risk.

# The LinchKey Reasoning Architecture

LinchKey does not begin with conclusions.

It begins by identifying what governs.

Everything that follows depends upon the governing authority.

The governing authority determines:

- which questions exist;
- which elements must be established;
- which exceptions apply;
- which dependencies exist;
- which evidence matters;
- and which conclusions are legally, technically, medically, scientifically, or professionally permitted.

The reasoning process therefore begins with authority—not conclusions.

---

## Step 1 — Find What Governs

Every module begins by identifying the controlling authority.

Examples include:

- constitutions;
- statutes;
- regulations;
- procedural rules;
- governing contracts;
- engineering standards;
- accounting standards;
- medical guidelines;
- scientific methodology;
- organizational policy;
- technical specifications;
- or any other controlling authority for the domain.

Without governing authority there is no governing pathway.

---

## Step 2 — Build the Required Questions

Once governing authority has been identified, LinchKey determines:

What must actually be established?

The engine does not begin with generic questionnaires.

It builds questions directly from governing authority.

Questions exist because governing authority requires them—not because they are commonly asked.

---

## Step 3 — Gather Verified Facts

Verified facts answer the required questions.

Unknown facts remain unknown.

Allegations remain allegations.

Professional opinions remain professional opinions.

Evidence is evaluated according to the governing authority rather than convenience or popularity.

---

## Step 4 — Build Governing Pathways

Each required element becomes its own pathway.

Each pathway may contain:

- required sub-elements;
- exceptions;
- alternate routes;
- timing requirements;
- procedural requirements;
- independent duties;
- downstream dependencies.

Every pathway remains independently visible.

Nothing is silently merged.

---

## Step 5 — Evaluate Dependencies

Many governing requirements depend upon earlier requirements.

One missing foundational element may prevent later conclusions from becoming established.

LinchKey evaluates every dependency separately before permitting downstream conclusions.

This reduces the risk that a single unsupported assumption silently changes an entire reasoning process.

---

## Step 6 — Explain Every Conclusion

Every conclusion must explain:

- what governs;
- why each question existed;
- what evidence supported each answer;
- which requirements passed;
- which requirements failed;
- which remain unresolved;
- which competing pathways were evaluated;
- what additional verified evidence could establish a different governing pathway;
- and which downstream conclusions depend upon the current result.

Reasoning that cannot be explained cannot be independently verified.

---

## Step 7 — Reanalyze Continuously

Reasoning is never frozen.

Whenever governing authority changes...

Whenever verified evidence changes...

Whenever required elements change...

Whenever new information becomes available...

LinchKey automatically reevaluates every affected pathway and every downstream conclusion.

Changing a conclusion because governing authority or verified evidence changed is correct behavior.

Failing to change after the governing pathway changed is reasoning error.

Law is one implementation.

Medicine is another.

Engineering is another.

Accounting is another.

Scientific research is another.

Cybersecurity is another.

The governing authority changes.

The reasoning architecture does not.

Because every professional discipline ultimately asks the same question:

# What Governs?

Every professional discipline has governing authority.

The governing authority may differ, but the reasoning architecture does not.

Law is governed by constitutions, statutes, regulations, procedural rules, controlling precedent, court orders, and other controlling legal authorities.

Medicine is governed by anatomy, physiology, pathology, pharmacology, evidence, standards of care, clinical guidelines, patient-specific findings, and applicable laws and regulations.

Engineering is governed by mathematics, physics, material science, engineering standards, building codes, design specifications, safety requirements, and applicable regulations.

Accounting is governed by accounting standards, financial regulations, auditing requirements, tax law, documentation, and reporting standards.

Scientific research is governed by methodology, reproducibility, evidence, peer review, statistical validity, ethics, and transparent analysis.

Cybersecurity is governed by technical standards, protocols, system architecture, organizational policy, regulatory requirements, threat models, and documented security controls.

Government agencies, businesses, educational institutions, investigators, insurers, and countless other organizations each operate within their own governing frameworks.

Although the governing authority changes, the reasoning architecture remains the same.

The first question is never:

"What do we think happened?"

The first question is:

**"What governs?"**

Only after the governing authority has been identified can LinchKey determine:

- what questions should be asked;
- what facts are required;
- what evidence is relevant;
- what procedures apply;
- what exceptions exist;
- what dependencies must be satisfied;
- what conclusions are permitted;
- and what remains unresolved.

Without first identifying what governs, later reasoning risks following assumptions instead of authority.

# Additional LinchKey Design Principles

The following principles further define the architecture and philosophy of LinchKey as the project continues to evolve.

## Governing Authority

In LinchKey, **governing authority** means the controlling sources applicable to a question.

Depending on the domain, those sources may include:

- constitutions;
- statutes;
- regulations;
- procedural rules;
- controlling decisions;
- court orders;
- contracts;
- professional standards;
- engineering codes;
- accounting standards;
- scientific methodology;
- technical specifications;
- organizational policy;
- clinical guidance;
- or other authoritative sources within the relevant field.

Authority is not limited to government.

It is whatever legitimately governs the question being evaluated.

## Traceability

Every material conclusion should remain traceable to the governing authority and verified evidence that support it.

Users should be able to inspect:

- which authority applied;
- why each question was asked;
- which facts were verified;
- which facts remained disputed or unknown;
- which requirements passed;
- which requirements failed;
- which alternative pathways were considered;
- and which downstream conclusions depended upon the result.

A conclusion that cannot be traced cannot be meaningfully verified.

## Beginning With the Correct Authority

Many reasoning failures occur not because the reasoning itself was careless, but because the analysis began from the wrong governing authority.

Once the wrong authority is selected, the system may ask the wrong questions, collect the wrong evidence, follow the wrong procedures, overlook required parties, miss exceptions, or reach conclusions that appear coherent while resting on the wrong foundation.

Finding the correct governing authority is therefore one of the most important steps in the entire reasoning process.

## Professional Judgment and Shortcut Risk

LinchKey is not designed to replace professional judgment.

It is designed to strengthen professional judgment by helping professionals verify that their conclusions continue to follow governing authority rather than habit, assumption, convenience, office practice, or unconscious shortcut reasoning.

Experience-based shortcuts can be useful.

They can also unintentionally bypass required elements.

An unsupported assumption may affect:

- notice;
- service;
- opportunity to be heard;
- procedural fairness;
- due process;
- evidence collection;
- diagnosis;
- safety;
- design compliance;
- financial reporting;
- investigative scope;
- regulatory duties;
- available remedies;
- and every later conclusion built upon the original error.

The danger is often not limited to one incorrect answer.

The danger is that one unsupported assumption can redirect the entire pathway.

## Proportional Uncertainty

LinchKey does not manufacture certainty.

Confidence should remain proportional to the governing authority and verified evidence supporting the conclusion.

Unknown facts remain unknown.

Conflicting facts remain visible.

Competing pathways remain open until they are supported, eliminated, or remain unresolved.

A confident presentation does not make incomplete reasoning complete.

## Transparent Reasoning

LinchKey is not designed to produce answers that merely appear convincing.

It is designed to produce reasoning that remains:

- transparent;
- inspectable;
- challengeable;
- correctable;
- reanalyzable;
- and continuously connected to what governs.

The objective is not to hide complexity.

The objective is to place complexity inside the engine where it can be organized, tested, and explained rather than forcing users to carry every dependency in their own memory.

## Complexity Belongs Inside the Engine

Users should not have to memorize every authority, exception, timing rule, dependency, evidentiary requirement, or alternate pathway before they can ask a meaningful question.

LinchKey carries that structural complexity while presenting the reasoning in a form that humans can inspect and understand.

The objective is not to make reasoning more complicated.

The objective is to make complexity visible where governing authority requires it, while making the reasoning easier to inspect, understand, and verify.

## Project Origin

LinchKey began from a recurring observation:

Many disagreements and professional failures do not begin because people refuse to reason.

They begin because different participants unknowingly start from:

- different assumptions;
- different governing authorities;
- different procedural pathways;
- different definitions;
- different versions of the controlling source;
- or different understandings of the available evidence.

Once those starting points diverge, later conclusions may appear irreconcilable even when each participant believes they are reasoning correctly.

LinchKey begins earlier.

It asks:

**What governs?**

From there, it determines what must be established, identifies which evidence is required, preserves what remains unknown, tests every pathway, and explains why the result follows—or why it does not.

**Every discipline has governing authority. Every conclusion should remain connected to it.**
